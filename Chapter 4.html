Nutritional Science Foundations

Modern nutrition science provides the evidence base for healthy eating principles. This section delves into the quality of macronutrients, optimal protein and fat sources, and the crucial roles of micronutrients, phytochemicals, and fiber.

Macronutrients & Carbohydrate Quality

Optimal diets balance macronutrients, emphasizing quality. Whole grains, vegetables, fruits, and legumes provide complex carbohydrates, fiber, vitamins, and minerals. High-quality carbohydrates improve glycemic control, lower cholesterol, and enhance satiety.

"Replacing refined white rice with brown rice or other whole grains can significantly improve nutrient intake in Taiwan."

Protein & Fat Sources

Adequate protein supports muscle, immune function, and satiety. TEA provides protein from seafood, soy products, legumes, and moderate poultry/eggs. Healthy fats from fish, nuts, seeds, and plant oils support cardiovascular health. Limiting saturated and avoiding trans fats are key.

Micronutrients, Phytochemicals & Fiber

Essential for metabolic and physiological functions. TEA's diverse ingredients ensure adequate intake. Phytochemicals (flavonoids, carotenoids, polyphenols) offer antioxidant/anti-inflammatory effects. Fiber supports digestive health, regulates blood glucose/lipid levels, and promotes satiety.

Evidence-Based Dietary Patterns

Numerous studies support dietary patterns emphasizing plant-based foods, healthy fats, and minimal processing:

  • Mediterranean & DASH diets: Reduce cardiovascular events, hypertension, mortality.
  • Plant-predominant diets: Lower stroke risk (Taiwanese cohorts).
  • Whole grains, nuts, fish: Reduce cardiovascular disease and all-cause mortality.
  • Conversely, high intake of sugar-sweetened beverages, processed meats, ultra-processed foods increases risk of diabetes, obesity, and mortality.

Healthy Taiwanese Eating Approach (TEA) - Core Components

The TEA pattern emphasizes greater consumption of certain food groups and cooking methods, while discouraging others:

  • Plant-based foods: Vegetables, fruits, whole grains, nuts, seeds, legumes. Encourages at least five servings of vegetables and fruits per day, with emphasis on leafy greens, cruciferous vegetables, and brightly colored fruits.
  • Soy products: Tofu, tempeh, soy milk, soy nuts. Provide high-quality protein and isoflavones.
  • Aquatic foods: Fish and seafood. Deliver omega-3 fatty acids, iodine, and high-quality protein. Each 20g/day increase in fish consumption associated with 4% reduction in cardiovascular mortality.
  • Tea: Green tea, oolong tea, Pu-erh tea. Rich in polyphenols, providing antioxidants that may lower blood pressure and improve endothelial function.
  • Whole grains, nuts, and seeds: Supply fiber, micronutrients, unsaturated fats. High whole-grain intake associated with 16% reduction in CVD. Additional 28g/day of nuts associated with 29% lower coronary heart disease.

Discouraged: Marbled or heavily processed meats, fried and high-fat foods, sugary snacks, sugar-sweetened beverages.

Preferred Cooking Methods: Boiling or steaming over deep-frying.
